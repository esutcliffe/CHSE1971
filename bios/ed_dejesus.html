3/17/03

Hi, everybody! I assume you're REALLY bored, if you're reading this...

After graduation, I went to URI, originally majoring in engineering. I quickly realized that engineering had way too many required courses, so I switched to the major with the absolute fewest required courses: math.
(Lesson: You can't plan everything. Be flexible.) When I got my bachelor's degree in math, I switched again, to physics, and got my master's in 1977. Most important classes taken: sailing and Shakespeare.

At the time, the only physics jobs available were in nuclear weapons design, so I went to work for IBM in Newport as a computer programmer. (Lesson: I can get jobs that, strictly speaking, I have no qualifications for.) IBM had a contract with the Navy to write software for the Trident submarine. I wrote code to help submarines not bump into things, like enemy torpedoes. I didn't really approve of the Trident program: at the same time I was working on this project, I was also actively protesting the building of the Trident. Go figure.

After 3 or 4 years, I suddenly remembered that I wasn't a computer programmer, but a physicist. My goal was to stay in IBM, but move to a job with more physics. IBM at that time had more money than it knew what to do with. Employee benefits were lavish. It was like a big, world-wide club. I thought my chances for physics opportunities would be better if I got my PhD, so I started taking courses part-time to finish my degree. (Lesson: Planning your life is like pushing a rope.)

In 1982, I went to a Fourth of July house party, invited by a guy I knew slightly who lived at the house. I met the most beautiful woman I've ever known. Bam! I was in love. I managed to spend as much time with her at the party as I could. Her name was Ellie McConnaughy, and she was smart and fun and nice. Two days later I asked her out on a date. We've been together ever since. (Lesson: Accept all invitations.)

Shortly afterwards, Ellie moved to New Haven for a year's experience as a research psychologist at a VA hospital. We commuted back and forth on weekends to see each other.

In 1983, I took a leave of absence from IBM to finish my doctorate. Ellie moved back to RI and finished up her doctorate. In 1984, Ellie had the opportunity for a two-year post-doctorate with a researcher she'd always wanted to work with. At Vanderbilt University. In Nashville, Tennessee. Gulp. We moved to Nashville, and I got a job teaching math and computers at a nearby state college (Middle Tennessee State University, in Murfreesboro).

We liked Tennessee. The weather was nice. The people were incredibly friendly. Of course, it was a little farther from the ocean than I prefer. And there was a bit of a language barrier, with my students understanding me and me understanding my students. We traveled around the area, to Atlanta, Louisville, Lexington, and the Smoky Mountains. (Lesson: I can probably live anywhere in the US, especially if catfish is available.)

In 1985, Ellie and I got married, back in RI, where most of our family and friends were. I also got my doctorate that year, after a mighty struggle: I had to do my dissertation defense three times before I passed. (Lesson: It's not a bad idea to find out how people are going to evaluate you before you get evaluated.)

In 1986, we moved back to New England, and settled in Norwood, MA to be near Boston and jobs. Ellie worked as a therapist at several clinics before starting her own private practice. We bought a house in Norwood in 1987.

I taught basic science at Boston University from 1986-1990. I didn't like it much. The students were too privileged and snotty. I took a job as a researcher at MIT Lincoln Labs in Lexington. Analyzing the trajectories of Soviet ballistic missile tests, if you can believe it. I was relieved to get laid off in 1993. (Lesson: Unemployment is God's way of telling you to stop working on defense.)

Our first child, James, was born in 1990. Yikes! Parenthood is quite an
experience: both joyful and fulfilling, and exhausting and frustrating. Our second child, Katie, was born in 1992. Our kids are really the core of our lives. Most of what we do revolves around providing a good and safe home, and all the love and support we can. I occasionally want to throttle them, like when they give me a hard time when I'm trying to help them with their homework. But there are moments that are simply wonderful, like seeing Katie do well at a swim meet, or watching James acting in a play. (Lesson: Kids are good. Who knew?)

After my blissful unemployment in 1993, I went freelance, helping high-tech companies write and test software, conducting training on technical topics, and producing documentation. (Lesson: I don't have to work for other people. I can make it on my own without much difficulty.)

On the basis of one product review I wrote for them, I got a job as an editor with prestigious BYTE magazine in 1994. I rapidly had to learn how to write and edit articles for a global magazine. Despite the way this bio sounds, I got the hang of it, and wrote a bunch of articles and cover stories for the magazine. BYTE went belly-up in 1998. My cover story on the coming Y2K problem appeared on the final issue.

In 1992, Ellie and I started going to church again. We had been members of the largest unorganized religion in the world, lapsed Catholics, but we felt we needed something more, especially if we wanted our children to experience religious thought. We attended a Unitarian church for a while, but it took us about six months to deduce that the minister believed in God. (I now think of Unitarianism as Religion Lite, a kind of halfway house for people abused by their religion of origin.) In 1995, Ellie began attending a church she found, and I went with her once. I was appalled. People were singing and clapping and calling things out. Very unruly. These people were Christians, with a capital C, and had a very strange set of beliefs. Still, Ellie liked it, so I went with her. I even started attending an informal class one night a week. I questioned the poor teacher mercilessly, tearing apart everything she was trying to present. I was extremely skeptical about the whole thing. Strangely, out of my skepticism, I developed certain beliefs, or rather realized that I did believe certain things. After a long period of thought and study, I became a Christian. Technically, I am a born-again Christian. Not like those morons you see on TV, who embarrass the life out of me. This is a tough religion: anything that starts out with "love your enemies" is not easy. But it has really changed my life. I know that some people have spiritual experiences, but I don't, or at least not the same kind. For me, Christianity gives perspective to my life and to the world.

After the demise of BYTE, I again turned freelance. I have written lots of articles for lots of magazines (try doing a Google search on Edmund DeJesus: yikes). I have done contract work for lots of high-tech companies, like EMC, CardScan, FutureTense (now history), and Baltimore Technologies. After my contract ended with Baltimore, they made me an offer I couldn't refuse to become an actual employee. I'm still with them (as of 2003). It's kind of nice having paid benefits and actual vacation days.

My life right now:
We still live in Norwood, MA. Ellie has a part-time private practice as a therapist, which she enjoys. She also devotes her time to several local projects, like a town anti-bullying committee. She's interested in developing her public speaking skills. She is a voracious reader and usually has a couple dozen books lined up to tackle. She loves to try out new recipes and I am an enthusiastic guinea pig. We try to go out at least a couple of times a month for little dates. I'm lucky to have her.

James is becoming a teenager, in years and behavior. He plays percussion in the junior high band. He loves acting, especially games and exercises in improv. He is very funny and original. He's perfectly relaxed onstage, which I find amazing. He is totally uninterested in anything that involves physical activity, and prefers to play Magic cards (if you don't know, don't even ask). It is fun to have a son I can introduce to the things I've always enjoyed. We like the same kinds of movies, mainly, and love seeing things like the Star Wars or Ring movies or Spiderman together.

Katie is a dynamo, with more energy than the rest of us put together. She finds it hard to sit still for more than about 30 seconds, and does her homework as if she were traversing an obstacle course. She is the tallest girl in her elementary school (thanks to Ellie's side of the family), which helps her stand out, but she's also beautiful: I am already looking at brochures for nice convents for her. She is a good singer and is in the town chorus. It's been really wonderful for me, a guy, to have a daughter, especially one as sweet and sensitive as she is. A few weeks ago, I heard her crying in her room. When I went to see what was wrong, she looked up from the book she was reading for school and said simply, "Winnie dies." Broke my heart.

I continue to write articles for magazines, mainly about computer topics, but I'm trying to write about science also. I don't think I'll ever get back to working in physics. Still, I love to read about astrophysics and particle physics and all that bizarre stuff that has nothing and everything to do with everyday life. I'm also trying to get some of my fiction writing published. I've published a short story and some poems, and self-published a mystery novel. I enjoy writing. I don't get to sail much, but love it when I do. I've found I enjoy learning about military strategy for some odd reason, and I'm working my way diligently through the Age of Empires computer game scenarios.

Last summer, I had to have heart surgery to repair a congenital valve problem. It was quite an experience to surrender myself to undergo something that I might not survive. It was also daunting to have to go through weeks of recovery when just walking to the corner is a real challenge: it's hard to be so incapable and vulnerable. It was a real eye-opener for me. I learned that I can surrender, and be weak, and be vulnerable, yet still survive, still be a man, a husband, a father. I will try to remember that twenty and thirty and forty years from now. (I'm perfectly healthy now: I've never been in such good shape.)

I don't think I ever really had a plan for my life, but even if I did, I know it hasn't gone according to any plan I could have devised. Who would have foreseen meeting Ellie? Who knew that I'd end up earning my living by writing, of all things? Who would have anticipated what having children would be like? It's all a surprise to me.
